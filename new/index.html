Assurance Engagement Framework

Definition An assurance engagement is one where a practitioner expresses a conclusion designed to enhance the degree of confidence of intended users (other than the responsible party) about the outcome of evaluating a subject matter against criteria.

Three-Party Relationship

Practitioner
The Auditor/CPA
Performs the engagement and expresses the conclusion
Responsible Party
Management
Prepares the subject matter information (e.g., FS)
Intended Users
Shareholders, Banks, etc.
Use the conclusion to make decisions

AT-02 · PSA Basics

Introduction to Auditing

AAA Definition of Auditing "A systematic process of objectively obtaining and evaluating evidence regarding assertions about economic actions and events to ascertain the degree of correspondence between those assertions and established criteria, and communicating the results to interested users." (American Accounting Association)

Types of Audit

TypePurposePerformed by
Independent FS AuditOpinion on fair presentation of FSExternal CPA/firm
Internal AuditOperational, management, financial reviewInternal auditors
Government AuditCompliance, FS, Performance (economy, efficiency, effectiveness)COA

Objective of FS Audit

To enable the auditor to express an opinion on whether the financial statements are prepared, in all material respects, in accordance with the applicable financial reporting framework.

The auditor does NOT: guarantee accuracy, ensure viability of the entity, nor assess stewardship. The auditor provides reasonable assurance only. Management is ALWAYS responsible for preparing the FS — audit does NOT relieve management of this responsibility.

Key Terms

Financial Statement Assertions
Representations by management, explicit or implicit, embodied in the FS. Used by auditors to consider types of potential misstatements.
Professional Skepticism
A questioning mind; critical assessment of evidence. Neither accept nor disbelieve — always probe.
Agency Theory
Managers have more info than shareholders → need for audit. Goal congruence issues arise.
Information Asymmetry
Users don't have the same info as management → audit bridges this gap.

Internal vs. External Auditor

External AuditorInternal Auditor
Reports toShareholders/members (outside governance)Board of directors & senior management (inside)
IndependenceIndependent from clientEmployee of entity
Primary objectiveOpinion on FSAdd value, improve operations
StandardsPSA / ISAIIA Standards

These are the assertions the auditor tests. Know them by category:

Existence vs. Completeness:
• Existence (overstatement): Start from the records → trace to supporting docs. Did this invoice really exist?

The auditor communicates results through the Auditor's Report (not the engagement letter or management letter). The medium for sharing audit results is always the audit report.

AT-06 · PSA 210 / 220

Audit Engagement & Pre-Engagement

Accepting an Audit Engagement

Before accepting, the auditor (engagement partner) evaluates 3 things:

  1. Relevant ethical requirements will be satisfied (especially independence)
  2. Sufficient competence, time, and resources are available
  3. Preconditions for audit exist — management acknowledges its responsibilities
Preconditions for an Audit Management must acknowledge: (a) responsibility for preparing FS in accordance with applicable FRF, (b) responsibility for internal control, and (c) provide the auditor with all information, access, and unrestricted access to persons.

Audit Engagement Letter (PSA 210)

Records the agreed terms of the audit engagement. Must include:

  • Objective and scope of the audit
  • Responsibilities of the auditor
  • Responsibilities of management
  • Identification of the applicable financial reporting framework
  • Expected form and content of the auditor's report
On recurring audits: the engagement letter need NOT be sent every year, but the auditor should assess if circumstances require revision. If there's a change in management, change in nature of the entity, or a reminder of the existing terms — renew or remind.

Change in Engagement Terms

When can the auditor NOT agree to change? If there is no reasonable justification for the change. Example: the client wants to downgrade from an audit to a compilation simply because the audit found errors — that is NOT acceptable. The purpose should not be to hide unfavorable results.

Initial Audit — Opening Balances (PSA 510)

The incoming auditor must obtain sufficient appropriate evidence that:

  • Opening balances do not contain material misstatements
  • Appropriate accounting policies are consistently applied

Methods: review predecessor's working papers, perform specific procedures, evaluate whether current-period procedures give evidence about opening balances.

PSA 220 — Documentation Requirements

  • Fulfillment of ethical requirements (including independence)
  • Acceptance and continuance conclusions
  • Nature and scope of consultations and conclusions
  • Confirmation that EQR (Engagement Quality Review) was completed before the audit report date

AT-07 · PSA 300 / 320

Audit Planning & Materiality

Audit Strategy vs. Audit Plan

Audit StrategyAudit Plan
LevelGeneral, high-levelSpecific, detailed
ContentScope, timing, direction, materiality, required communicationsRAP, further audit procedures (TOC + substantive), other PSA-required procedures
SequenceEstablished FIRSTDeveloped AFTER strategy
FormatMemorandumWritten (required by PSA)
The audit strategy is NOT more detailed than the audit plan — it's the other way around. Plan = detailed; Strategy = general. This is a common trap question!

Materiality (PSA 320)

Key Principle Information is material if its omission or misstatement could reasonably be expected to influence the economic decisions of users.
TermDefinitionRelationship
Overall Materiality
(FS as a whole)		 The threshold set for the FS as a whole Largest amount
Performance Materiality Amount set at LESS than overall materiality to reduce the probability that aggregate uncorrected + undetected misstatements exceed overall materiality Less than overall materiality
Specific Materiality Set for specific classes of transactions or disclosures (e.g., related party transactions) May be lower than overall
If Overall Materiality = ₱1,000,000 → Performance Materiality might be set at ₱700,000.
Why? So the combined effect of individually immaterial misstatements doesn't accidentally exceed the ₱1M threshold. Think of it as a "safety buffer."

Materiality vs. Audit Risk — Inverse Relationship

Higher Materiality → Lower Audit Risk → Less Evidence Needed Lower Materiality → Higher Audit Risk → More Evidence Needed

Purpose of Audit Planning

  • Ensure appropriate attention to important areas
  • Identify potential problems early
  • Assign team members appropriately
  • Direct and supervise team effectively
  • Coordinate with component auditors (group audits)

Required Documentation (Audit Planning)

  1. Audit Strategy
  2. Audit Plan
  3. Significant changes to the audit strategy/plan and reasons for changes
  4. Overall Materiality, Performance Materiality, any revisions

AT-08 / AT-10 · PSA 315 / 330

Audit Risk Formula & Components

AR = IR × CR × DR Audit Risk = Inherent Risk × Control Risk × Detection Risk
DR = AR ÷ (IR × CR) Detection Risk is the DEPENDENT variable — auditor controls this

The Three Risk Components

ComponentDefinitionWho controls it?Direction of Relationship with DR
IR — Inherent Risk Susceptibility of an assertion to material misstatement, BEFORE considering controls Client (inherent to the business) IR ↑ → DR ↓ (auditor does more work)
CR — Control Risk Risk that a material misstatement will NOT be prevented or detected by internal controls Client (strength of internal controls) CR ↑ → DR ↓ (auditor does more work)
DR — Detection Risk Risk that the auditor's procedures will FAIL to detect a material misstatement Auditor (by nature, timing, extent of procedures) Independent variable is RMM; DR is set inversely
IR and CR are functions of the CLIENT and its environment — the auditor cannot change them, only assess them. DR is the only risk the auditor can MANIPULATE by changing the nature, timing, and extent of procedures.

Risk of Material Misstatement (RMM)

RMM = IR × CR Assessed at Financial Statement Level AND Assertion Level

Risk Relationship Matrix

If IR is…And CR is…Then ADR should be…Meaning: Auditor should…
HighHighLowestPerform the MOST procedures
HighMediumLowerPerform many procedures
MediumMediumMediumModerate procedures
LowLowHigherPerform fewer procedures
CPALE-type Question: If IR = HIGH and CR = MEDIUM, what is the acceptable level of DR?

Answer: LOWER (because RMM is high, DR must be set low, meaning the auditor must perform extensive substantive procedures to keep overall AR at an acceptably low level.)

Significant Risks

Significant Risk = A risk of material misstatement where inherent risk assessment is close to the UPPER END of the spectrum. For significant risks:
• Substantive procedures ALONE cannot provide sufficient appropriate evidence
• Auditor MUST perform tests of controls
• Must perform specific substantive procedures designed for that significant risk

Higher Risk = More Procedures. Know these high-risk accounts:

High Inherent Risk
Cash, Held-for-trading securities, Accounts receivable, Revenue, Related party transactions
Lower Inherent Risk
Property, plant & equipment (routine; less susceptible to theft), long-term liabilities

AT-09 · PSA 315 (Revised)

Internal Control — COSO Framework

Internal Control Definition A process designed, implemented and maintained by those charged with governance, management, and other personnel to provide reasonable assurance (NOT absolute) about achieving objectives related to: financial reporting, operations, and compliance.

5 Components of Internal Control (CRIME)

🔑 Mnemonic — CRIME
C-R-I-M-E
Control Environment · Risk Assessment (entity's) · Information & Communication · Monitoring Activities · Entity's Control Activities
ComponentNatureWhat to Know
Control Environment Indirect controls Tone at the top; management philosophy; ethical values; organizational structure; HR policies. FOUNDATION of IC.
Risk Assessment Indirect controls The entity's own process to identify and respond to risks. Different from auditor's risk assessment!
Information & Communication Indirect controls Information system captures, processes, and reports transactions accurately. Must communicate relevant info to the right people.
Monitoring Activities Indirect controls Ongoing evaluation of IC quality. Includes internal audit, management reviews.
Control Activities Direct controls The actual policies and procedures that ensure management directives are carried out. Most directly testable.
Two types of components:
• Indirect controls: Control Environment, Risk Assessment, Information & Communication, Monitoring → affect the overall control environment
• Direct controls: Control Activities → directly prevent/detect/correct misstatements at the assertion level

Limitations of Internal Control

Why IC can NEVER give absolute assurance:
1. Human judgment in decision-making
2. Breakdowns (errors, mistakes)
3. Collusion — even segregation of duties can be circumvented
4. Management override of controls
5. Cost-benefit constraint

Types of Control Activities

  • Preventive controls — stop errors/fraud before they happen (e.g., requiring authorization)
  • Detective controls — find errors/fraud after they happen (e.g., reconciliations, audits)
  • Corrective controls — fix identified issues (e.g., error correction procedures)

Controls can also be: Manual (human performed), Automated (system performed), or IT-dependent manual (rely on IT output).

Segregation of Duties (SOD)

Incompatible functions must be separated:

  • Authorization — approving a transaction
  • Recording — recording the transaction in books
  • Custody — physical custody of assets
The cashier (custody) should NOT also record cash receipts (recording). If one person controls both, they can steal cash and cover it up by falsifying the records.

Key Concepts in Understanding IC (Auditor's Work)

  1. Understand the design of controls (is the control designed well?)
  2. Determine if they are implemented (is the control actually in use?)
  3. Assess operating effectiveness (is the control working consistently?) → tested through Tests of Controls

Control Deficiencies

TypeDefinitionCommunication to
Control DeficiencyControl doesn't exist, or is not designed/operating properlyManagement (optional communication)
Significant DeficiencyImportant enough to merit attention by TCWGThose Charged with Governance (required)
Material WeaknessSignificant deficiency; reasonable possibility of material misstatement NOT being prevented/detectedTCWG (required, timely)

AT-11 · PSA 330

Risk Assessment Procedures & Tests of Controls (TOC)

Three Types of Audit Procedures

Procedure TypePurposeWhen
Risk Assessment Procedures (RAP)Understand the entity and its environment + IC to identify & assess RMMPlanning phase
Tests of Controls (TOC)Test OPERATING EFFECTIVENESS of controlsAfter RAP; if auditor plans to rely on controls
Substantive TestsDetect material misstatements in account balances, transactions, and disclosuresThroughout the audit

Risk Assessment Procedures (RAP)

The three RAP procedures are:

🔑 Remember: I-A-O
I - A - O
Inquiries (of management, TCWG, internal audit, in-house counsel, employees) · Analytical Procedures (develop expectations about plausible relationships) · Observation and Inspection (plant tours, reading documents, inspecting records)
Inquiry ALONE is NOT sufficient for RAP. The auditor must CORROBORATE through observation, inspection, and analytical procedures. Inquiry is the weakest form of evidence.

Tests of Controls (TOC)

When to perform TOC:
1. When the auditor PLANS to rely on the operating effectiveness of controls in determining the nature, timing and extent of substantive tests; OR
2. When substantive procedures ALONE cannot provide sufficient appropriate audit evidence (i.e., for significant risks)

TOC Procedures:

🔑 Mnemonic: I-O-I-R
I - O - I - R
Inquiry · Observation · Inspection of documents/records · Reperformance

TOC vs. Substantive Testing — Effect of Result

TOC ResultEffect on Control RiskEffect on Substantive Testing
Controls are EFFECTIVEControl Risk ↓ (Low)Substantive testing can be REDUCED
Controls are NOT EFFECTIVEControl Risk ↑ (High)Substantive testing must be INCREASED

Key Cycle Controls to Know

CycleKey DocumentsKey Control Objective
Revenue/SalesSales order, Delivery receipt, Sales invoice, Official receiptOccurrence: All recorded sales actually happened
Expenditure/APPurchase requisition, Purchase order, Receiving report, Vendor invoiceAP Dept. verifies by matching PO + Receiving Report + Vendor Invoice (3-way match)
CashBank statements, Cash receipts journal, Deposit slipsCustodian ≠ recorder (segregation of duties)
The accounts payable department verifies propriety of acquisitions by comparing: Purchase Order + Receiving Report + Vendor Invoice (the "3-way match"). This is a classic control over Expenditure Cycle.

AT-12 · PSA 330 / 500

Substantive Testing & Audit Evidence

What is Substantive Testing?

Substantive procedures are designed to detect material misstatements at the assertion level in account balances, classes of transactions, and disclosures.

Two Types of Substantive Procedures

TypeIncludesGood for…
Tests of Details • Tests of transactions
• Tests of balances		 Specific assertion testing; tracing, vouching, confirmations, physical inspection
Analytical Procedures
(Substantive APs)		 Trend analysis, ratio analysis, reasonableness tests Identifying unusual relationships; supporting tests of details

Analytical Procedures — 3 Uses

StagePurposeRequired?
PlanningDirects attention to likely misstatements; helps develop RAP✅ REQUIRED
SubstantiveSupports or refutes account balancesOptional (auditor's choice)
Overall ReviewReviews reasonableness of account balances near completion✅ REQUIRED

Sufficient & Appropriate Evidence

Sufficiency
QUANTITY
More risk = more evidence needed. Higher quality evidence = less quantity needed.
Appropriateness
QUALITY
Evidence is appropriate when it is both RELEVANT (relates to the assertion) and RELIABLE.

Reliability Hierarchy of Evidence

More Reliable → Less Reliable:

  1. Evidence from independent external sources (e.g., bank confirmation)
  2. Evidence obtained directly by the auditor (e.g., physical count by auditor)
  3. Evidence from entity's records when internal controls are effective
  4. Evidence obtained indirectly or by inference
  5. Evidence from original documents > photocopies/faxes
The weakest evidence is oral testimony (inquiry). The strongest is direct physical evidence obtained by the auditor. Documentary evidence from external sources (bank confirmations) is generally more reliable than internally-generated documents.

Timing of Substantive Procedures

Detection Risk LevelTesting Timing
Low DR (high RMM)Year-end testing preferred; more extensive work
High DR (low RMM)May perform interim testing (before year-end)

Audit Documentation (Working Papers)

Working papers are the record of:

  • Audit procedures performed
  • Relevant audit evidence obtained
  • Conclusions reached by the auditor
File TypeContentsRetention
Current FileCurrent year audit plan, audit programs, working trial balance, management representation letter5 years from date of auditor's report
Permanent FileLong-term contracts, articles of incorporation, organization charts, prior year audited FSIndefinitely
No deletions of audit documentation are allowed after the assembly period (60 days after the date of the auditor's report). After assembly, the file is locked — no additions, changes, or deletions.

AT-14 · PSA 240 / PSA 250

Fraud, Error & Non-Compliance

Fraud vs. Error

FraudError
IntentINTENTIONALUNINTENTIONAL
ExamplesFraudulent financial reporting, misappropriation of assetsArithmetic mistakes, misapplication of accounting policies
Auditor's concernWhen it causes material misstatement in FSAny misstatement, particularly material ones

Two Types of Fraud

Fraudulent Financial Reporting
Management manipulates the FS (overstatement of revenues, understatement of liabilities). E.g., channel stuffing, premature revenue recognition.
Misappropriation of Assets
Theft of assets (skimming cash, pilfering inventory, lapping). Usually by lower-level employees.

Fraud Risk Triangle

🔑 Fraud Triangle
I - O - R
Incentive/Pressure (need or motivation to commit fraud) · Opportunity (weak internal controls) · Rationalization (justifying the act: "I'll pay it back")

Responsibilities — Who Does What?

PartyResponsibility
Management + TCWGPrimary responsibility for prevention and detection of fraud
AuditorResponsible for obtaining reasonable assurance that FS are free from material misstatement due to fraud OR error; maintain professional skepticism throughout
The auditor does NOT make legal determinations of whether fraud has occurred — that is for law enforcement/courts. The auditor only identifies and assesses the risk of material misstatement due to fraud.

Key PSA 240 Requirements

  1. Maintain attitude of professional skepticism
  2. Discussion among engagement team about susceptibility to fraud
  3. Inquire of management: process for identifying fraud risks, awareness of any actual/suspected fraud
  4. Inquire of TCWG: how it oversees fraud risk management
  5. Evaluate unusual/unexpected analytical procedure relationships (especially revenue accounts)
  6. Consider whether identified fraud risks are significant risks

Presumed Significant Fraud Risks (Always)

Two always-present significant risks (PSA 240):
1. Revenue recognition — always presumed a significant risk (management may be incentivized to manipulate revenue)
2. Management override of controls — always a significant risk; no matter how good the controls, management can circumvent them

PSA 250 — Non-Compliance with Laws and Regulations

CategoryAuditor's Approach
Laws directly affecting FS (e.g., tax, pension)Obtain sufficient appropriate audit evidence of compliance
Other laws (environmental, employment)Perform specified procedures; inquire of management; stay alert for indications of non-compliance
The risk of NOT detecting fraud is higher than the risk of not detecting an error, because fraud involves deception (forgery, collusion, deliberate failure to record). Procedures effective for errors may NOT be effective for fraud.

Responses to Identified Fraud Risk

  • Assign more experienced staff to high-risk areas
  • Increase unpredictability in audit procedures (change timing, add surprise procedures)
  • Address management override risk: always test journal entries, especially non-standard ones, and year-end adjustments
  • Include retrospective review of accounting estimates for possible bias

AT — Computation Items

The 13 Computation Items

Instructor's Note These 13 computation items regularly appear in the CPALE. Memorize the formulas and the logic behind them. Work through each example carefully — these can be the difference between passing and conditional.

1. Audit Risk Model

AR = IR × CR × DR
Given: AR = 5%, IR = 80%, CR = 60%
DR = AR ÷ (IR × CR)
DR = 0.05 ÷ (0.80 × 0.60)
DR = 0.05 ÷ 0.48
DR = 10.42%
Higher IR + CR → Lower DR → Auditor must do MORE substantive testing

2. CPALE Rating Computation

Passing rules: General Average ≥ 75%, No subject below 65% = PASSED.
GA ≥ 75% but one subject 60–64% = CONDITIONAL.
GA < 75% OR any subject below 60% = FAILED.
Candidate A: 88, 82, 79, 76, 62, 85
Average = (88+82+79+76+62+85) ÷ 6 = 472 ÷ 6 = 78.67%
Average ≥ 75 ✓ BUT Subject 5 = 62 (60–64 range)
Result: CONDITIONAL — must retake Subject 5 and score ≥ 75

3. Detection Risk — Inverse Relationship

ADR = AR ÷ RMM = AR ÷ (IR × CR)
Scenario: IR = High (100%), CR = High (100%), AR = 5%
ADR = 0.05 ÷ (1.0 × 1.0) = 5% (Lowest possible DR)
Auditor must perform maximum substantive testing

4. Materiality Benchmarks

BaseTypical % RangeBest for…
Total revenues0.5% – 1%Profit entities
Total assets0.5% – 1%Balance sheet heavy entities
Net income / profit before tax5% – 10%Investor-focused entities
Total expenditures0.5% – 1%Non-profit, government
Equity1% – 5%Financing decisions
Total Assets = ₱50,000,000; Benchmark = 1%
Overall Materiality = ₱50,000,000 × 1% = ₱500,000
Performance Materiality = 70% × ₱500,000
Performance Materiality = ₱350,000

5. Audit Sampling — Sample Size Relationships

Factor Increases (↑)Effect on Sample Size
Risk of material misstatement (IR, CR)Increases ↑
Tolerable misstatement / Tolerable deviation rateDecreases ↓
Expected error / deviation rateIncreases ↑
Confidence level (1 - DR)Increases ↑
Population sizeIncreases ↑ (slightly)
Acceptable level of DR (higher DR)Decreases ↓
Tolerable misstatement is the maximum misstatement an auditor can accept and still conclude the balance is fairly stated. Higher tolerable misstatement = fewer samples needed.

6. Tolerable Misstatement vs. Performance Materiality

Tolerable Misstatement ≤ Performance Materiality ≤ Overall Materiality
Overall Materiality = ₱500,000
Performance Materiality = ₱350,000 (70% of overall)
Tolerable Misstatement per account = set at or below ₱350,000

7. Effect of Assessed Risk on Evidence

Lower DR required → More substantive evidence needed
Higher DR acceptable → Less substantive evidence needed
Strong controls (low CR) → Can plan for higher DR → Less substantive testing
Inverse: DR and (IR × CR) are INVERSELY related

8. Lower Risk = Lower Sample Size

Auditor assessed IR = Low; controls effective (CR = Low)
RMM = Low → ADR = High → Less substantive testing
Fewer confirmations needed; can accept higher tolerable deviation

9. Projected Misstatement

Projected Misstatement = (Sample Misstatement ÷ Sample Book Value) × Population Book Value
Sample book value = ₱200,000; Sample misstatement found = ₱4,000
Population book value = ₱2,000,000
Projected misstatement = (₱4,000 ÷ ₱200,000) × ₱2,000,000
Projected misstatement = ₱40,000

10. Deviations in TOC (Tests of Controls)

Deviation Rate = Number of Deviations ÷ Sample Size
Sample size = 100; Deviations found = 7
Deviation rate = 7 ÷ 100 = 7%
Tolerable deviation rate = 5%
7% > 5% → Controls CANNOT be relied upon
Increase substantive testing; do NOT reduce DR based on controls

11. Confidence Level & Risk of Assessing CR Too Low

Confidence Level = 1 − Risk of Assessing CR Too Low (Risk of over-relying on controls that are actually ineffective)
Confidence level = 95%
Risk of assessing CR too low = 1 - 0.95 = 5%

12. Overall Audit Risk — Acceptable Level

Goal: Keep overall AR ≤ 5% (standard)
If IR = 60%, CR = 50%:
DR = 0.05 ÷ (0.60 × 0.50) = 0.05 ÷ 0.30
Acceptable DR = 16.67%

13. CPALE Score — Conditional Credit System

Conditional candidate: passed 5 subjects; failed 1 (scored 62)
Conditional credits for the 5 passed subjects are RETAINED
Must retake only the 1 failed subject
Must score ≥ 75 on the retake within the allowed period
If achieved: PASSED the CPA Board Exams
For TOC computations: if deviation rate found > tolerable deviation rate → rely less/not at all on controls → increase substantive testing. For substantive testing: if projected misstatement > tolerable misstatement → FS is likely materially misstated → qualify or expand procedures.

Conceptual Framework Approach

Instead of listing rules for every scenario, the Code uses a conceptual framework:

  1. Identify threats to the fundamental principles
  2. Evaluate the significance of the threat
  3. Address the threat (apply safeguards or eliminate the circumstance)
The Reasonable and Informed Third Party test: Would a reasonable person, knowing all the facts, conclude that the accountant has complied with the fundamental principles? This is the key test for evaluating whether a threat has been adequately addressed.

Independence — Key Definitions

Independence in Mind
The actual state — not allowing external factors to influence judgment. Real independence.
Independence in Appearance
How it looks to others — avoiding facts and circumstances that a reasonable third party would find objectionable.

Confidentiality — When CAN You Disclose?

  • With client's permission
  • When required by law or regulation (e.g., court order, anti-money laundering)
  • To comply with quality review or professional inquiry
  • To protect the professional's interests in legal proceedings
Confidentiality survives even after the client relationship ends. A CPA cannot disclose confidential client information just because the engagement is over.

Self-Test

Quick Quiz — Test Yourself!

Try to answer before clicking "Show Answer."

Q1: What is the correct audit risk formula?
AR = IR × CR × DR. Detection Risk = AR ÷ (IR × CR). DR is the only component the auditor can control.
Q2: A practitioner expresses: "Nothing has come to our attention that causes us to believe the FS are materially misstated." What type of assurance is this?
Limited Assurance — expressed in NEGATIVE form. This is the conclusion given in a REVIEW engagement (PSRE).
Q3: A candidate's CPALE scores are: 80, 79, 77, 76, 62, 88. Did they pass?
Average = (80+79+77+76+62+88)/6 = 462/6 = 77%. GA ≥ 75 ✓, but Subject 5 = 62 (in the 60–64 range) → CONDITIONAL. Must retake Subject 5 and score ≥ 75 to pass completely.
Q4: Which component of internal control is considered the "foundation" and mainly an indirect control?
Control Environment — it sets the "tone at the top" and includes management philosophy, ethical values, and organizational structure. It is an INDIRECT control.
Q5: Under PSA 240, which two fraud risks are ALWAYS presumed to be significant risks?
1. Revenue recognition (management may inflate revenues), and 2. Management override of controls (management can always circumvent even the best internal controls).
Q6: If IR = 80% and CR = 75%, and the auditor wants to keep AR at 5%, what should DR be?
DR = AR ÷ (IR × CR) = 0.05 ÷ (0.80 × 0.75) = 0.05 ÷ 0.60 = 8.33%. The auditor must design procedures so detection risk does not exceed 8.33%.
Q7: What are the 5 fundamental principles of the Code of Ethics?
IOPCB: Integrity, Objectivity, Professional Competence & Due Care, Confidentiality, Professional Behavior.
Q8: What is the difference between Audit Strategy and Audit Plan?
Audit Strategy = General, high-level approach (scope, timing, direction). Audit Plan = Specific, detailed set of procedures (RAP, TOC, substantive tests). Strategy is established FIRST. The plan is MORE detailed than the strategy — a common trap question!

Top 10 Exam Traps

  1. "Audit guarantees accuracy" → FALSE. Audit provides HIGH but NOT ABSOLUTE assurance.
  2. "Auditor is responsible for fraud prevention" → FALSE. Management & TCWG are primarily responsible. Auditor detects, not prevents.
  3. "Audit strategy is more detailed than audit plan" → FALSE. Plan is more detailed.
  4. "DR is a function of the client" → FALSE. DR is controlled by the AUDITOR.
  5. "High IR means high DR" → FALSE. High IR → LOW DR (more work needed).
  6. "IC provides absolute assurance" → FALSE. IC provides REASONABLE assurance only.
  7. "Collusion cannot happen if duties are segregated" → FALSE. Collusion CAN bypass SOD.
  8. "Auditor makes legal determinations of fraud" → FALSE. Courts do. Auditor only assesses risk.
  9. "COR expires every 3 years" → FALSE. COR does NOT expire. PIC expires every 3 years.
  10. "Inquiry alone is sufficient evidence" → FALSE. Must corroborate inquiry with observation/inspection.
